Industrial Noise: What It Is, Why It's Dangerous, and How to Control It

By Mecart Engineering Team·Updated March 2025·15 min read
HERO IMAGE — INDUSTRIAL ENVIRONMENT

Industrial noise is unwanted sound generated by machinery, equipment, and manufacturing processes that exceeds safe exposure levels. Any sustained noise above 85 dBA is considered hazardous by OSHA, NIOSH, and the WHO. It causes irreversible hearing loss, reduces workplace productivity by up to 34%, and costs U.S. employers $242 million per year in compensation claims.

KEY TAKEAWAYS
  • Industrial noise above 85 dBA causes irreversible hearing loss and costs U.S. employers $242 million/year in compensation claims (NIOSH).
  • Noise reduces problem-solving ability by 34% and increases errors, fatigue, and employee turnover.
  • OSHA limit: 90 dBA / 8 hours. NIOSH recommends 85 dBA. Canada: 87 dBA (ESDC).
  • Engineering controls (acoustic enclosures, control rooms, operator cabs) are the most effective fix, reducing noise 20–39 dBA.
  • No single method guarantees protection. A layered approach is essential.

#Key Characteristics of Industrial Noise You Need to Understand

Industrial noise has six key characteristics that determine how dangerous it is and how to control it: intensity (dB), frequency (Hz), duration, vibration, propagation, and directionality. Understanding these is essential before choosing a control solution.

📊Noise Levels (dB)

Industrial noise ranges from moderate (~60–70 dB) to extreme (100+ dB). Prolonged exposure above 85 dB causes hearing damage. Above 120 dB, harm is immediate. Every 10 dB increase is perceived as roughly twice as loud.

🔊Frequency (Hz)

Low-frequency noise travels long distances and penetrates barriers, making it the hardest to control. High-frequency noise is easier to block with acoustic panels.

⏱Duration Patterns

  • Continuous: Conveyors, ventilation, compressors
  • Intermittent: Drilling, stamping, batch processing
  • Impulsive: Hammering, pneumatic tools, explosions
DID YOU KNOW?

Sound doesn't increase linearly. Every 10 dB increase is perceived as twice as loud. 100 dB isn't "a bit more" than 90 dB. It's double. (Source: CDC/NIOSH)

#Industrial Noise Regulations: OSHA, NIOSH, WHO, and Canadian Standards

The U.S. limit is 90 dBA/8hrs (OSHA), NIOSH recommends 85 dBA, Canada's federal limit is 87 dBA. Sustained exposure above these thresholds causes permanent hearing damage.

  • PEL: 90 dBA over 8-hour TWA
  • Action Level: 85 dBA triggers Hearing Conservation Program
  • Exchange Rate: 5 dB — every 5 dB increase halves allowable time
  • REL: 85 dBA over 8-hour TWA
  • Exchange Rate: 3 dB — stricter than OSHA
  • Basis: Based on risk of material hearing impairment over working lifetime
  • Recommended limit: 85 dBA over 8-hour TWA
  • Context: Global guideline to minimize hearing loss risk
  • Note: 1.1 billion people at risk of hearing loss worldwide
  • Federal limit: 87 dBA over 8-hour TWA
  • Employer obligation: Monitor, implement controls, provide PPE when exceeded
  • Note: Provincial regulations may be stricter

#How to Control Industrial Noise: The Hierarchy of Solutions

The most effective approach follows a hierarchy. No single method guarantees protection. A layered strategy is essential.

❌1. Eliminate at the SourceMost effective▼
Remove the noise source entirely or redesign the process. When operations depend on heavy machinery, move to the next level.
🔄2. Substitute Equipment▼
Replace noisy machinery with quieter models. Use alternative materials or processes. Often the best cost-benefit option.
🛡3. Engineering ControlsRecommended▼
Address noise at the source or create barriers protecting workers. The most effective long-term solution.
  • Acoustic enclosures around loud machinery
  • Modular control rooms and operator cabs isolating workers
  • Noise barriers between source and work areas
  • Industrial silencers on ventilation and exhausts
📋4. Administrative Controls▼
Schedule noisy tasks during off-hours. Rotate employees through quieter assignments. Limits exposure time, not the noise itself.
🎧5. PPELast resort▼
Earmuffs and earplugs are the last line of defense, not the first. Depends on individual compliance, proper fit, and consistent use. Should supplement, not replace, other controls.

HIERARCHY OF CONTROLS PYRAMID

WHY ENGINEERING CONTROLS WIN

PPE protects one person and depends on compliance. An acoustic enclosure protects everyone, every shift, zero reliance on individual behavior.

#FAQ: Industrial Noise Control

What is a safe noise level?+
OSHA: 90 dBA/8hrs. NIOSH/WHO: 85 dBA. Canada: 87 dBA. Most professionals align with 85 dBA.
Most effective way to reduce noise?+
Engineering controls. Enclosures, control rooms, and operator cabs protect everyone without relying on PPE compliance.
How much can enclosures reduce noise?+
20–39 dBA depending on STC rating. STC 45 = 39 dBA reduction. STC 35 = 25 dBA reduction.
Health effects of industrial noise?+
Hearing loss, cardiovascular issues, chronic stress, cognitive decline, sleep disorders. Costs employers $242M/year.
U.S. vs Canada regulations?+
OSHA: 90 dBA (5 dB exchange). NIOSH: 85 dBA (3 dB). Canada ESDC: 87 dBA. Provincial rules may be stricter.
Engineering controls vs PPE?+
Engineering controls protect everyone permanently at the source. PPE protects one person and depends on compliance. OSHA/NIOSH prefer engineering controls.
